Why GaN is Reshaping Power Electronics in 2026

Gallium Nitride is unlocking smaller, cooler, and faster power stages. Here's what PCB designers need to know about layout, thermal management, and choosing GaN drivers.

By ChipKraft Team· Apr 28, 2026· 6 min read

What is GaN and Why Now?

Gallium Nitride (GaN) transistors have existed in research labs for decades, but only in the last few years has the technology become affordable enough for mainstream power supply design. Unlike silicon MOSFETs, GaN devices operate at much higher switching frequencies with dramatically lower switching losses — meaning smaller inductors, smaller capacitors, and a smaller overall power stage.

In 2026, GaN is no longer a niche component. It's appearing in USB-C chargers, server PSUs, EV on-board chargers, and industrial motor drives. If you design power electronics boards, you need to understand how to lay out GaN devices correctly.

Key Advantages Over Silicon

  • Higher switching frequency — typically 100kHz–10MHz vs. 20–100kHz for silicon
  • Lower on-resistance (RDS(on)) — less conduction loss at the same die area
  • Near-zero reverse recovery — eliminates one of silicon's biggest loss mechanisms
  • Higher temperature operation — GaN handles higher junction temps without performance cliff
  • Smaller system size — magnetics shrink dramatically as frequency rises

"A GaN-based 65W charger can fit in a form factor that would take three times the volume with a silicon design."

PCB Layout Rules for GaN

GaN's speed is both its superpower and its challenge. Fast switching edges mean parasitic inductance that was invisible in a silicon design becomes a serious problem with GaN. Here are the critical layout rules:

1. Minimise Gate-Source Loop Area

The gate drive loop — from gate driver output, through the gate resistor, to the GaN gate pin, through the source pin, and back — must be as small as possible. Even a few nanohenries of parasitic inductance causes ringing that can exceed the gate oxide breakdown voltage.

💡 Rule of Thumb: Keep the gate drive loop area under 0.5 cm². Place the gate resistor within 5mm of the gate pin, on the same layer.

2. Kelvin Source Connection

Many GaN gate drivers support a Kelvin source pin (separate from the power source). Always use it. It eliminates the source inductance feedback that causes instability in the gate drive circuit at high switching speeds.

3. Decouple the Bus Locally

Place high-frequency ceramic decoupling capacitors (100nF–1µF, X7R, rated for your bus voltage) as close to the drain-source switch node as possible — ideally directly underneath the device in a via-in-pad design. A 10µF or 100µF bulk capacitor should be within 10–15mm.

Thermal Management

GaN devices are typically packaged in small QFN or LGA footprints with an exposed pad on the bottom. This is the primary heat escape path. Your layout must support it:

  • Use a solid copper thermal pad under the device, connected to an internal ground plane via thermal vias (at least 4–9 vias, 0.3mm drill, 0.15mm annular ring)
  • Do not use solder mask on the thermal pad area
  • Consider a 2oz copper inner layer for the ground plane if power dissipation exceeds 2W
  • For high-power applications (>10W), a heatsink or metal-core PCB may be required

Choosing a GaN Gate Driver

Standard silicon MOSFET drivers are usually unsuitable for GaN. They lack the speed and protection features needed. Look for these in a GaN-specific driver:

  • Propagation delay under 20ns — matched rise and fall times prevent shoot-through
  • Under-voltage lockout (UVLO) on both rails
  • Negative gate drive capability — driving the gate to -3V to -5V during off-state prevents spurious turn-on
  • Integrated bootstrap diode for half-bridge configurations

Getting Started

GaN is not harder to use than silicon — it just requires more discipline in layout and driver selection. The rewards are real: smaller boards, better efficiency, and the ability to reduce your BOM by shrinking passive components.

When creating your GaN device footprint, precise pad dimensions and courtyard placement are critical.
