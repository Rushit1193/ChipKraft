HASL vs ENIG vs OSP: Picking the Right Surface Finish

Surface finish affects solderability, shelf life and cost. We break down the trade-offs for prototype and production runs so you can make the right call every time.

By ChipKraft Team·Apr 04, 2026·5 min read

Why Surface Finish Matters

Bare copper oxidises within hours of exposure to air. The surface finish is the protective coating applied to exposed copper pads after fabrication — it keeps the copper solderable until your boards go through reflow or wave soldering. The wrong finish can cause cold joints, poor wettability, or added cost with no benefit.

HASL — Hot Air Solder Levelling

HASL is the traditional workhorse finish. The board is dipped in molten solder (tin-lead or lead-free) and excess is removed with hot air knives, leaving a solder coating on all exposed pads.

  • Cost: Very low — cheapest finish available
  • Shelf life: 12 months
  • Solderability: Excellent — the pad is already pre-tinned
  • Flatness: Poor — the surface is uneven, unsuitable for fine-pitch components under 0.5mm
  • Best for: Through-hole boards, hobby projects, large-pitch SMD (0805 and above)

⚠️ RoHS Note: Traditional HASL uses tin-lead solder. For RoHS-compliant products, specify Lead-Free HASL (LF-HASL) — slightly higher cost but compliant.

ENIG — Electroless Nickel Immersion Gold

ENIG deposits a layer of nickel (3–6µm) followed by a thin layer of gold (0.05–0.1µm). The nickel is the solderable layer; the gold protects it from oxidation until soldering.

  • Cost: Medium-high — typically 2–3× more than HASL
  • Shelf life: 12 months+
  • Solderability: Excellent and very consistent
  • Flatness: Excellent — ideal for fine-pitch QFN, BGA, and 0402 or smaller
  • Best for: Production boards, fine-pitch SMD, boards with edge connectors or gold fingers

"ENIG is the default choice for any board with components under 0.5mm pitch — the flat surface is non-negotiable for reliable paste printing."

OSP — Organic Solderability Preservative

OSP is a thin organic coating (0.2–0.5µm) applied to bare copper. It's transparent and environmentally friendly, but burns off during reflow — which is the point.

  • Cost: Very low — comparable to HASL
  • Shelf life: 6 months (shorter than HASL or ENIG)
  • Solderability: Good for first reflow; degrades with multiple heat cycles
  • Flatness: Excellent — as flat as bare copper
  • Best for: High-volume production with controlled storage, single-reflow assemblies

Which Should You Choose?

For most designs: ENIG. It works for prototypes and production, handles all component pitches, and has a long shelf life. The extra cost is negligible compared to assembly labour.

Use HASL only for simple through-hole or large-pitch boards where cost is the primary concern. Use OSP for high-volume production where you control storage conditions and assemble quickly.
