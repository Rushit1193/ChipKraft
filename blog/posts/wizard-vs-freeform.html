From Wizard to Freeform: Choosing the Right Footprint Workflow

A practical comparison of guided footprint builders versus full freeform editors, with real-world examples from QFN to BGA packages.

By ChipKraft Team·Apr 18, 2026·8 min read

Two Ways to Build a Footprint

Every PCB designer eventually faces the footprint problem: the component you need isn't in any library, or the library version doesn't match your manufacturer's land pattern recommendation. When that happens, you have two paths — use a guided wizard, or build it freeform from scratch.

Both approaches have their place. Knowing when to use each one saves hours of rework and prevents costly manufacturing errors.

The Wizard Approach

Wizard-based builders walk you through a structured form: enter pin count, pitch, body dimensions, and the tool generates a compliant footprint automatically. Chipkraft's Wizard Builder uses IPC-7351 rules to calculate pad sizes, courtyard clearances, and silkscreen positions from your inputs.

When to use a Wizard

  • Standard packages — QFP, QFN, SOIC, SOT, SOP, BGA with regular pitch
  • Speed matters — prototype boards where you need a footprint in under 2 minutes
  • IPC compliance required — the wizard handles density level (A/B/C) calculations automatically
  • Less experienced designers — the guided flow prevents common mistakes like wrong courtyard or missing fab layer

💡 Pro Tip: Always cross-check wizard output against the component datasheet land pattern recommendation. Wizards use IPC formulas which may differ from manufacturer-specific recommendations for thermal or RF performance.

The Freeform Approach

Freeform editing means placing every pad, line, and arc manually using a coordinate-based editor. It gives you complete control but requires a deeper understanding of PCB design rules.

When to use Freeform

  • Non-standard packages — custom connectors, edge-mount components, unusual pad shapes
  • Manufacturer-specific land patterns — when the datasheet specifies exact dimensions that differ from IPC
  • RF and high-frequency components — where pad geometry directly affects impedance
  • Mechanical components — mounting holes, heatsinks, shields with complex outlines

Side-by-Side Comparison

Here's how the two approaches compare across common design scenarios:

  • QFN-32, 0.5mm pitch → Wizard wins. IPC formula gives correct thermal pad and paste aperture in seconds.
  • BGA-256, 0.8mm pitch → Wizard wins. Manual placement of 256 pads is error-prone; the wizard places them in a perfect grid.
  • Custom USB-C receptacle → Freeform wins. The mixed through-hole/SMD pad arrangement requires manual placement.
  • RF SMA connector → Freeform wins. Impedance-matched pad geometry must follow the datasheet exactly.

"Use the wizard for 80% of components — save freeform for the 20% that are truly non-standard."

The Hybrid Workflow

The most efficient approach is hybrid: start with the wizard to generate the base footprint, then switch to freeform editing to make specific adjustments. This gives you IPC-compliant starting dimensions with the flexibility to fine-tune pad shapes, add custom fab notes, or adjust courtyard clearances for dense layouts.

Conclusion

Neither approach is universally better. The wizard saves time on standard packages and ensures IPC compliance. Freeform editing gives you control for non-standard situations. Master both, and you'll never be blocked by a missing footprint again.
